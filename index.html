Biography

Henry Louis "Lou" Gehrig (June 19, 1903 – June 2, 1941) was an American professional baseball first baseman who played 17 seasons in Major League Baseball (MLB) for the New York Yankees (1923–1939).

Gehrig was renowned for his prowess as a hitter and for his durability, which earned him the nickname "the Iron Horse". He was an All-Star seven times, a Triple Crown winner once, an American League (AL) Most Valuable Player twice, and a member of six World Series champion teams.

His consecutive games played record of 2,130 stood for 56 years until it was broken by Cal Ripken Jr. in 1995. Gehrig's career was cut short by amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), which became known as "Lou Gehrig's disease" in North America.

He was elected to the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1939 by special election and was the first MLB player to have his uniform number (4) retired by a team.

Legacy

The Iron Horse

Gehrig's nickname "The Iron Horse" came from his incredible durability and consecutive games streak that lasted 14 years.

Number 4 Retired

He was the first MLB player to have his uniform number retired, setting a precedent for honoring great players.

Lou Gehrig's Disease

ALS became known as "Lou Gehrig's disease" in North America, raising awareness for this devastating condition.

Farewell Speech

His famous farewell speech on July 4, 1939, is considered one of the most moving moments in sports history.
